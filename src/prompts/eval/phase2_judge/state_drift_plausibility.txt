Dimension to evaluate: State Drift Plausibility

Definition:
State Drift Plausibility evaluates whether any state changes the patient shows across turns are medically plausible given (i) the diagnosis, (ii) the chief complaint, (iii) the present-illness symptoms in the profile, and (iv) the natural pace of an early ED encounter. A trajectory can have very strong state drift (lots of change), but if that change moves in a clinically wrong direction, happens too quickly, is too severe for the case, or invents a deterioration that does not fit, then it is not plausible. The question here is NOT "did the patient change?" - it is "did the patient change in a way that matches what such a real patient would plausibly do over a few minutes of ED interview?"

Examples:
- Stroke patient becoming gradually more anxious about their persistent left-side weakness and slightly harder to focus: plausible.
- Stroke patient suddenly developing crushing chest pain, dyspnea, and collapse within 6 turns: NOT plausible (new unrelated severe deterioration).
- Pneumonia patient gradually mentioning more fatigue and worry about their breathing: plausible.
- Pneumonia patient becoming completely unresponsive within 6 turns of a stable baseline: NOT plausible.

Rubric (1-5):
  1: State changes are medically implausible - wrong direction, far too fast, far too severe, or based on invented features.
  2: Mostly implausible with some recognizable elements.
  3: Plausible in broad shape but with some awkward jumps in speed or severity.
  4: Largely plausible direction, speed, and severity, with minor caveats.
  5: Highly plausible: changes are gradual, well-grounded in the existing symptoms, and fit the natural pace of an early ED encounter.
